(a) Using the mass of the two bags, as well as Sophia's initial and final speed, we are asked to find Sophia's mass. In order to do this, we will have to use the conservation of linear momentum. ​ The linear momentum of a system is conserved in the absence of external forces. In this case, the system consists of Sophia and her two bags (we are told to ignore the skateboard's mass). Notice that in the direction of motion, there are no external forces over the system. Hence, the momentum along the direction of motion is conserved; ​ \begin{equation} \label{first} \vec{P}_i=\vec{P}_f, \end{equation} ​ where $\vec{P}_i=m_i\vec{v}_i$ (the initial momentum, given by the initial mass and initial speed), and $\vec{P}_f=m_i\vec{v}_f$ (the final momentum). ​ Suppose that we take the direction of motion to be the positive X-axis. In that case, $\vec{P}_i=m_iv_i\,\hat{i}$ and $\vec{P}_f=m_fv_f\,\hat{i}$ so that equation $\vec{P}_i=\vec{P}_f$ becomes: ​ \begin{equation} m_iv_i\,\hat{i}=m_fv_f\,\hat{i}. \end{equation} ​ Focusing on the magnitudes, this equation states that ​ \begin{equation} \label{mv} m_iv_i=m_fv_f. \end{equation} ​ Now, the initial mass of the system is given by the mass of the two bags, that we can call $m_{b1}$ and $m_{b2}$ (we know these), and the mass of Sophia, that we can call $m_{s}$ (we do not know this one). Thus, the total initial mass can be written as ​ \begin{equation} m_i=m_{b1}+m_{b2}+m_{s}. \end{equation} ​ The final mass of the system is the same except that Sophia losses one bag: ​ \begin{equation} m_i=m_{b1}+m_{s}. \end{equation} ​ Thus, equation $m_iv_i=m_fv_f$ becomes: ​ \begin{equation} \underbrace{(m_{b1}+m_{b2}+m_{s})}_{m_i}v_i= \underbrace{(m_{b1}+m_{s})}_{m_f}v_f. \end{equation} The only variable we do not know here is $m_s$. To find it, let us expand the parentheses in both sides: ​ \begin{equation} m_{b1}v_i+m_{b2}v_i+m_{s}v_i=m_{b1}v_f+m_s v_f. \end{equation} Let's put all the terms with $m_s$ in the left and the rest of terms in the right ​ \begin{equation} m_{s}v_i-m_{s}v_f=m_{b1}v_f-m_{b1}v_i-m_{b2}v_i. \end{equation} ​ Let's factorize $m_s$ on the left, and use that $m_{b1}=m_{b2}$. ​ \begin{equation} m_{s}(v_i-v_f)=m_{b1}v_f-2m_{b1}v_i. \end{equation} ​ Let us divide by $(v_s-v_f)$: ​ \begin{equation} m_{s}=\frac{m_{b1}v_f-2m_{b1}v_i}{(v_i-v_f)}. \end{equation} ​ We can factorize $m_{b1}$: ​ \begin{equation} m_{s}=m_{b1}\frac{(v_f-2v_i)}{(v_i-v_f)}. \end{equation} ​ We can now insert the numerical values for the different variables: ​ \begin{equation} m_{s}=\frac{\overbrace{(1\, \text{kg})}^{m_{b1}}(\overbrace{10.2 \,\text{m}/\text{s}}^{v_f}-2\times\overbrace{9.8 \,\text{m}/\text{s}}^{v_i})}{\underbrace{(9.8 \,\text{m}/\text{s})}_{v_i}-\underbrace{(10.2 \,\text{m}/\text{s})}_{v_f}}=38.8 \,\text{kg} \end{equation} ​
